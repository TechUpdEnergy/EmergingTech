How Nuclear Fusion Works

Nuclear fusion is the process that powers the sun and stars. It involves forcing light atomic nuclei (typically isotopes of hydrogen, such as Deuterium (D) and Tritium (T)) to combine or "fuse" together at extremely high temperatures and pressures, forming a heavier nucleus (helium) and releasing a tremendous amount of energy in the process.

Major Fusion Projects

üá∫üá∏ National Ignition Facility (NIF), LLNL (USA)

In December 2022, NIF achieved scientific energy gain (ignition), producing more energy from a fusion reaction than was delivered by the lasers to the fuel target. This milestone was repeated multiple times with even higher energy yields through 2023 and 2024, providing crucial validation for ICF. Reports in early 2025 highlight ongoing experiments to further understand and optimize ignition.

üåç ITER (International Thermonuclear Experimental Reactor, France)

A massive international collaboration to build the world's largest tokamak, designed to demonstrate sustained fusion reactions with a significant net energy gain (Q=10) for extended periods. As of early 2025, ITER is in its advanced assembly phase. While facing revised timelines (e.g., a 2024 update suggested initial operations around 2035), it remains a central project for fusion science and engineering.
