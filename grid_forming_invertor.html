Next-Generation Grid Stability and Control Technology

Challenge Addressed

As traditional synchronous generators (which provide physical inertia to the grid) are replaced by inverter-based resources (IBR) like solar, wind, and batteries, the grid loses inertia, making it more susceptible to frequency fluctuations and instability.

Grid Inertia Loss = Higher Frequency Instability Risk
Traditional generators retiring faster than ever before

üá¶üá∫ Hornsdale Power Reserve (South Australia) - Tesla Megapack

While initially known for its grid-following capabilities, strategic upgrades and specific operational modes for the Tesla battery system at Hornsdale have demonstrated groundbreaking grid-forming functionalities. The system now provides synthetic inertia and significantly improves grid stability in a region with exceptionally high wind penetration. Multiple projects throughout 2023-2024 focused on enabling and optimizing these advanced capabilities, making it a global reference for grid-forming technology.

üèùÔ∏è Hawaii's Path to 100% Renewable Energy (USA)

Hawaiian Electric has been at the forefront of grid-forming technology adoption, actively collaborating with inverter manufacturers and renewable energy developers to incorporate grid-forming capabilities into new solar and battery projects. This is essential for ensuring grid stability as Hawaii pursues its ambitious goal of 100% renewable energy. A flagship example is Maui's Kuihelani Solar + Storage project (60MW solar, 240MWh storage) by AES, which came online in 2024 and incorporates cutting-edge grid-forming technology as a cornerstone of the renewable energy transition.
