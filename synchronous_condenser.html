Mechanical Inertia and Reactive Power Support

Challenge Addressed

The retirement of traditional synchronous generators (coal, gas, nuclear) and the increasing penetration of Inverter-Based Resources (IBR) like solar, wind, and batteries reduce the overall rotational inertia in the power system. Low inertia makes the grid more susceptible to rapid frequency deviations (high Rate of Change of Frequency - RoCoF) and instability during disturbances.

How It Works

A synchronous condenser (also known as a synchronous compensator or SynCon) is essentially a large synchronous generator that operates without a prime mover (like a turbine) and is not connected to any load to produce net active power.

Its rotor is brought up to synchronous speed with the grid,
typically by a pony motor or static frequency converter (SFC)

Modern synchronous condensers are often equipped with advanced digital control systems and can sometimes be coupled with flywheels to further augment their inertial response.

Reactive Power Control

By adjusting the DC excitation current in its rotor field winding, a synchronous condenser can either generate reactive power (when over-excited, acting like a capacitor) or absorb reactive power (when under-excited, acting like a reactor). This allows for dynamic voltage regulation on the transmission grid.

Inertia Provision

The significant rotating mass of its rotor, spinning in synchrony with the grid, provides physical (mechanical) inertia. This inertia naturally resists changes in grid frequency, helping to slow down RoCoF during sudden generation/load imbalances and giving other control systems more time to react.
